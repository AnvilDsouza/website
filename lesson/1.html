Prologue — New Brief, Old Patterns

I arrived on a rain-sweet morning and the building smelled faintly of coffee and committee fatigue. Triad Finance had hired me because they wanted machine intelligence that produced clarity — not confusion. The CEO's slide read: Project Phoenix — Predict churn by Q3. Deliver impact. I had twenty-one unread messages and one very clear instruction: align everyone or we fail loudly.

Turn the page — I’m ready to start Project Phoenix.
Chapter 1 — The Ask

I was shown into a glass-walled room where the CEO, Vikram, was already mid-gesture. He likes gestures; they make his decisions feel theatrical. “We want churn prediction,” he said, as if the words were currency. “I don't want a prototype—show me impact.” Beside him, Rohit (CTO) tapped the vendor deck on his tablet like a metronome. Maya, head of data, looked exhausted but politely hopeful. Neha from product marketing asked whether the model should trigger retention campaigns or inform pricing.

My first move mattered. If I invited everyone into a single kickoff, I might get alignment but also a febrile room of competing demands. If I spoke with key stakeholders one-on-one, I might build strong alliances but lose perceived transparency. The vendor rep had a demo scheduled for tomorrow—fast results on a silver platter.

A — Run the vendor demo tomorrow. Speed matters; we’ll show quick impact and worry about alignment later.
B — Do one-on-one stakeholder interviews first. Build trust and listen; speed can follow.
C — Call a short kickoff with all stakeholders but frame the session as discovery: no decisions, only questions.
Chapter 2A — The Vendor Mirage

I let Rohit push the vendor demo. The theatre of product shows is intoxicating: slick slides, pseudo-science charts, and a demo that predicted churn for a sample dataset with jaw-dropping accuracy. The board liked the numbers. I tasted victory—until integration started.

The vendor model was black-boxed and required PII which our compliance team flagged. Worse: when we ran it against live data, performance tanked. The board wanted answers. Maya wanted time. I had to choose whether to double-down with a co-built contract or cut ties and build internal capability.

A — Negotiate a co-build with the vendor (shared accountability). Keep their momentum and buy engineering time.
B — Cancel and build internally. Slow, but we control the data and privacy.
Chapter 2B — The Quiet Work

I took Maya and Neha aside that afternoon. Maya walked me through label drift: churn labels weren’t churn; they were billing quirks and seasonal noise. Neha told stories of losing customers because of heavy-handed retention emails. Through individual conversations, I built a map: goals, fears, incentives.

That evening I had a smaller list of clear, conflicting objectives—and two possible operational moves: design a narrow MVP for high-value users, or demand a broad product that satisfied marketing and sales at the risk of complexity.

A — Define a narrow MVP: target the high-value segment with a simple intervention.
B — Scope broadly to appease Marketing and Sales. Bigger rollout, faster top-line buzzy numbers.
Chapter 2C — The Roundtable

The discovery kickoff was an awkward dance of competing agendas. I asked questions and wrote them on a board, prioritizing impact, speed, and risk. There was heat, but no decisions. I left with clearer problem statements and an uneasy sense that the board expects visible progress.

Now I had to choose whether to formalize a quick pilot with basic safeguards, or take the team through a formal discovery sprint that might calm the room but slow the calendar.

A — Run a short discovery sprint: structured interviews, prototypes, and user validation.
B — Start a pilot quickly to show progress; we'll patch the governance later.
Chapter 3E — Contracts & Compromises

Negotiating a co-build is a political beard-trimming exercise. Legal wants indemnities; vendor asks for exclusivity. I offered a tight SLA, data isolation, and co-developed explainability modules. The vendor agreed reluctantly. We gained speed but took on complexity—and a soft sharing of blame.

Months later, a production incident revealed an unfair score against a minority segment. Customers complained. Now, with smoke in the air, I must choose how to respond publicly.

A — Quiet patch and proceed; the board wants no headlines.
B — Publicly disclose, apologize, and open a third-party audit.
Chapter 3F — Building the House

We cancelled the vendor and started building a small in-house model. It was messy. There were late nights, spicy conversations, and a growing sense of craftsmanship. We designed the pipeline with privacy-first principles. The first prototype was modest but honest.

The board initially frowns at the slower pace; yet when the prototype is interpretable and conservative in decisions, the compliance team nods. A choice appears: push for a tighter MLOps program (governance & automation), or accelerate feature scope to please revenue now.

A — Invest in MLOps and governance; this is a long game.
B — Prioritize product features and iterate — show value quickly.
Chapter 3C — The MVP Revelation

We launched a tiny pilot focused on a high-LTV segment. The intervention was a gentle retention nudger — timely, tailored, and reversible. The initial metrics looked promising: engagement up, retention slightly better, and marketing finally had a case study.

But now the temptation: institutionalize this small success across all segments, or keep it narrow and refine? One leads to broad exposure and faster headlines; the other builds a stable, explainable base.

A — Systematize what worked: build playbooks and a conservative scaling plan.
B — Scale quickly and apply the intervention to all customers.
Chapter 3D — The Feature Avalanche

We said "yes" to many features. The roadmap turned into a Christmas tree of requests: retention emails, pricing experiments, predictive product recommendations. Engineering velocity frayed. Meetings multiplied. People started telling me stories of more meetings than work.

I can try a hard reset with the leadership (painful but clarifying) or keep accommodating and hope the engineers find their groove.

A — Call a candid roadmap reset and demand a single north star metric.
B — Keep accommodating — momentum is political currency.
